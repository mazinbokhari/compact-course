Some basic terms to start off... A <span style="color: #007fff;">solution</span> is made up of a <span style="color: #007fff;">solute</span> dissolved in a <span style="color: #007fff;">solvent</span>. A solute can be thought of as the substance with less mass or the part that is dissociated in the other substances. A solvent is the substance with more mass, or the dissolver of the solution.

<h2 style="text-align: center;"><span style="color: #007fff;">Dissociation and Properties that Affect It</span></h2>
One rule affects how things dissociate, which means to break into ions or monomers/ions. This is called "<span style="color: #007fff;">like dissolves like</span>." Due to the many intermoleular forces at work, solutes with similar polarity as their solvents will dissociate in them. Like polarities are dissociable. The same goes for nonpolar solutes in nonpolar solvents.

Another thing that affects dissociation and more so solubility is <span style="color: #007fff;">van't Hoff factor (i)</span>. This tells us how many ions a solute molecule will break up into. Solutes that cant be dissociated are given a factor of 1, and the property increases up the scale depending on the number of ions it breaks up into.

<h2 style="text-align: center;"><span style="color: #007fff;">Solubility and Equilibrium</span></h2>

Most of the time with problems involving <img alt="K_{sp}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?K_{sp}" align="absmiddle" /> have to do with a salt.

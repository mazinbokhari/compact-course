<h2 style="text-align: center;"><span style="text-decoration: underline;">Graphic Relationships with Calculus</span></h2>
<span style="color: #ff0000;">Recall </span>
<ol>
	<li><span style="color: #ff0000; line-height: 14px;">The velocity graph plots the slope of the displacement graph at every single point</span></li>
	<li><span style="color: #ff0000;">The acceleration graph plots the slope of the velocity graph at every single point</span></li>
	<li><span style="color: #ff0000;">The area bounded by the curve above the x axis for the velocity graph equals the total distance traveled in the positive direction</span></li>
	<li><span style="color: #ff0000;">The area bounded by the curve below the x axis for the velocity graph equals the total distance traveled in the negative direction</span></li>
	<li><span style="color: #ff0000;">The area bounded by the curve above the x axis for the acceleration graph equals the amount of positive change in velocity</span></li>
	<li><span style="color: #ff0000;">The area bounded by the curve below the x axis for the acceleration graph equals the amount of negative change in velocity</span></li>
</ol>
<h3 style="text-align: center;">Displacement and Velocity</h3>
We learned that <img alt="v = \frac{\mathrm{d} x}{\mathrm{d} t}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?v&amp;space;=&amp;space;\frac{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;x}{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;t}" align="absmiddle" />. What this means is that the velocity function is the derivative of the displacement function. This can be seen as
<p style="text-align: center;"><img alt="{\color{Blue} v(t) = \frac{\mathrm{d} x}{\mathrm{d} t} }\, \, \, or\, \, \, {\color{Blue} v(t)={x}'(t)}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{\color{Blue}&amp;space;v(t)&amp;space;=&amp;space;\frac{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;x}{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;t}&amp;space;}\,&amp;space;\,&amp;space;\,&amp;space;or\,&amp;space;\,&amp;space;\,&amp;space;{\color{Blue}&amp;space;v(t)={x}'(t)}" align="absmiddle" /></p>

<ul>
	<li><span style="line-height: 14px;">This is the reason why 1. above is true because the derivative function plots the slope of another function at every point
</span></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/07/x-and-v-slopes.png"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4357" style="margin: 5px auto; display: block; text-align: center;" alt="x and v slopes" src="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/07/x-and-v-slopes.png" width="700" /></a></p>
We learned that <img alt="x=\int v\, \, \mathrm{d} t" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?x=\int&amp;space;v\,&amp;space;\,&amp;space;\mathrm{d}&amp;space;t" align="absmiddle" />. What this means is that the displacement function is the antiderivative of the velocity function. This can be seen as
<p align="center"><img alt="{\color{Blue} x(t)=\int v(t)\, \, \mathrm{d} t}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{\color{Blue}&amp;space;x(t)=\int&amp;space;v(t)\,&amp;space;\,&amp;space;\mathrm{d}&amp;space;t}" align="absmiddle" /></p>
<p align="center"><span style="color: #007fff;">where v(t) is the velocity function and x(t) is the displacement function</span></p>

<ul>
	<li>This is the reason why 3. and 4. above are true because the antiderivative function plots the area bounded above or below the x axis and the curve of another function</li>
</ul>

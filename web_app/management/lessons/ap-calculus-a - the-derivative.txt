<h2 style="text-align: center;"><span style="line-height: 24px; text-align: start;">Tangent vs Secant</span></h2>
<span style="text-decoration: underline; color: #007fff;">Tangent Line:</span> The straight line that runs through a point on the graph and plots the slope of the graph at that point

<span style="text-decoration: underline;"><span style="color: #007fff; text-decoration: underline;">Secant Line:</span></span> The straight line that runs through two different points on the graph
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/tan-and-sec.png"><img class="size-full wp-image-5068 aligncenter" alt="tan and sec" src="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/tan-and-sec.png" width="373" height="291" /></a></p>

<h2 style="text-align: center;">Average Rate of Change (AROC)</h2>
<span style="text-decoration: underline; color: #007fff;">Average Rate of Change:</span> The slope of the secant line through two points on a graph
<ul>
	<li><span style="line-height: 14px;">The average rate of change can be found with the equation below</span></li>
</ul>
<img class="aligncenter" alt="{\color{Blue} AROC=\frac{\Delta f(x)}{\Delta x}=\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{\color{Blue}&amp;space;AROC=\frac{\Delta&amp;space;f(x)}{\Delta&amp;space;x}=\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}}" align="absmiddle" />
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/AROC-1.png"><img class="size-full wp-image-5069 aligncenter" alt="AROC 1" src="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/AROC-1.png" width="444" height="302" /></a></p>

<ul>
	<li> If we substitute x for a+h, where h=x-a, another form of the equation that we will use becomes</li>
</ul>
<img class="aligncenter" alt="{\color{Blue} AROC=\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{\color{Blue}&amp;space;AROC=\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}}" align="absmiddle" />
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/AROC-2.png"><img class="size-full wp-image-5070 aligncenter" alt="AROC 2" src="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/AROC-2.png" width="470" height="296" /></a></p>

<h2 style="text-align: center;">Instantaneous Rate of Change (IROC) and the Derivative</h2>
<span style="text-decoration: underline; color: #007fff;">Instantaneous Rate of Change:</span> The slope of the tangent line through one point on a graph

This is also known as the <span style="color: #007fff;">derivative</span> of a function at a point

<span style="text-decoration: underline;"><span style="color: #007fff; text-decoration: underline;">Derivative:</span></span> How much a function is changing at a point
<h3 style="text-align: center;">Notation for the Derivative</h3>
There are many notations for which we symbolize the derivative of a function. These are seen below for the function y = f(x).

<img class="aligncenter" alt="{f}'(x)={f}'={y}'=\frac{\mathrm{d} f}{\mathrm{d} x}=\frac{\mathrm{d} y}{\mathrm{d} x}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{f}'(x)={f}'={y}'=\frac{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;f}{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;x}=\frac{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;y}{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;x}" align="absmiddle" />
<h3 style="text-align: center;">Finding the Derivative with Limits</h3>
Say we want to find the derivative of a function at a point, point P. If another point, point Q, lies on the same function, the secant line between the two does not look like the tangent line to point P unless points P and Q are very close. This is seen with the graphs below.
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/sec-becomes-tan-1.png"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-5077" alt="sec becomes tan 1" src="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/sec-becomes-tan-1.png" width="363" height="290" /></a><a href="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/sec-becomes-tan-2.png"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-5078" alt="sec becomes tan 2" src="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/sec-becomes-tan-2.png" width="354" height="292" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/sec-becomes-tan-3.png"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-5079" alt="sec becomes tan 3" src="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/sec-becomes-tan-3.png" width="348" height="290" /></a><a href="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/sec-becomes-tan-4.png"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-5080" alt="sec becomes tan 4" src="http://www.compactcourse.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/sec-becomes-tan-4.png" width="368" height="294" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: left;">As we can see, when P and Q become very close as h approaches 0, the secant and tangent lines look identical. This means that the average rate of change becomes the instantaneous rate of change (the derivative). So when we take the limit of the average rate of change as h approaches 0, we can find the derivative at the point.</p>

<ul>
	<li><span style="line-height: 14px;">This can be seen with the equation</span></li>
</ul>
<img class="aligncenter" alt="{\color{Blue} {f}'(x)=\lim_{h\rightarrow 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{\color{Blue}&amp;space;{f}'(x)=\lim_{h\rightarrow&amp;space;0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}}" align="absmiddle" />
<p style="text-align: center;">This is the definition of the derivative!</p>
<p style="text-align: left; padding-left: 30px;">ex.) Find <img alt="{f}'(x)" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{f}'(x)" align="absmiddle" /> if <img alt="f(x)=x^{2}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?f(x)=x^{2}" align="absmiddle" />.</p>

	<li><img alt="{\color{Red} {f}'(x)=\lim_{h\rightarrow 0}\frac{x^{2}+2xh+h^2-x^{2}}{h}}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{\color{Red}&amp;space;{f}'(x)=\lim_{h\rightarrow&amp;space;0}\frac{x^{2}+2xh+h^2-x^{2}}{h}}" align="absmiddle" /></li>
	<li><img alt="{\color{Red} {f}'(x)=\lim_{h\rightarrow 0}\frac{h(2x+h)}{h}=\lim_{h\rightarrow 0}2x+h=2x}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{\color{Red}&amp;space;{f}'(x)=\lim_{h\rightarrow&amp;space;0}\frac{h(2x+h)}{h}=\lim_{h\rightarrow&amp;space;0}2x+h=2x}" align="absmiddle" /></li>

<h3 style="text-align: center;">Chain Rule</h3>
When we are taking the derivative of a function that has a function within it, we use the <span style="color: #007fff;">Chain Rule</span>. It states that to find the derivative of a composite function, we find the derivative of the outside function first, then multiply it by the derivative of the inside function as seen below.
<p style="text-align: center;"><img alt="{\color{Blue} {(f(g(x)))}'={f}'(g(x))\cdot {g}'(x)}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{\color{Blue}&amp;space;{(f(g(x)))}'={f}'(g(x))\cdot&amp;space;{g}'(x)}" align="absmiddle" /></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">If we substitute u for g(x), this can be rewritten as</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img alt="{\color{Blue} {(f(g(x)))}'={f}'(u)\cdot {g}'(x)}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{\color{Blue}&amp;space;{(f(g(x)))}'={f}'(u)\cdot&amp;space;{g}'(x)}" align="absmiddle" /></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Another variation for the Chain Rule can be written as</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img alt="{\color{Blue} \frac{\mathrm{d} y}{\mathrm{d} x}=\frac{\mathrm{d} y}{\mathrm{d} u}\cdot \frac{\mathrm{d} u}{\mathrm{d} x}}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{\color{Blue}&amp;space;\frac{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;y}{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;x}=\frac{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;y}{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;u}\cdot&amp;space;\frac{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;u}{\mathrm{d}&amp;space;x}}" align="absmiddle" /></p>

We can substitute this to get <img alt="{\color{Red} \left ( u\right )^{5}}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{\color{Red}&amp;space;\left&amp;space;(&amp;space;u\right&amp;space;)^{5}}" align="absmiddle" />.</span>

<h3 style="text-align: center;">Product Rule</h3>

<h2 style="text-align: center;">Differentiability and Continuity for Derivatives</h2>
If a function <img alt="f" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?f" align="absmiddle" /> is differentiable (has a derivative) at <img alt="x= c" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?x=&amp;space;c" align="absmiddle" />, then <img alt="f" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?f" align="absmiddle" /> is continuous at <img alt="x= c" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?x=&amp;space;c" align="absmiddle" />.
<ul>
	<li><span style="line-height: 14px;">This stems from the definition of the derivative with <img alt="{f}'(c)" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{f}'(c)" align="absmiddle" /> as</span></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img alt="{f}'(c)=\lim_{x\rightarrow c}\frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c}" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{f}'(c)=\lim_{x\rightarrow&amp;space;c}\frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c}" align="absmiddle" /></p>

<ul>
	<li><span style="line-height: 14px;">So if the derivative <img alt="{f}'(c)" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?{f}'(c)" align="absmiddle" /> exists, then <img alt="\lim_{x\rightarrow c}f(x)=f(c)" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?\lim_{x\rightarrow&amp;space;c}f(x)=f(c)" align="absmiddle" /> which means <img alt="f" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?f" align="absmiddle" /> is continuous at <img alt="x= c" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?x=&amp;space;c" align="absmiddle" />
</span></li>
	<li>This means that there cannot be a hole, jump, vertical asymptote, vertical tangent, corner, or cusp at <img alt="x= c" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?x=&amp;space;c" align="absmiddle" /></li>
</ul>
<h2 style="text-align: center;">The Mean Value Theorem</h2>
<span style="text-decoration: underline; color: #007fff;">Mean Value Theorem:</span> If a function <img alt="f(x)" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?f(x)" align="absmiddle" /> is
<ol>
	<li><span style="line-height: 14px;">continuous at every point on the closed interval <img alt="[a, b]" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?[a,&amp;space;b]" align="absmiddle" />

	<li>every point has a derivative on the open interval <img alt="(a, b)" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?(a,&amp;space;b)" align="absmiddle" /></li>
</ol>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">then there is at least number <img alt="c" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?c" align="absmiddle" /> such that <img alt="a&lt; c&lt; b" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?a&lt;&amp;space;c&lt;&amp;space;b" align="absmiddle" /> where</p>
